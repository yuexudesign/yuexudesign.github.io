Work in the United States (WITUS)

Team: Priya Gupta, Yeon Soo Park, Yue Xu, Qinwei Zhu, Kenzie Zou

My role: user research / UX design / usability testing / visual design

Problem Context

In 2016, there were about 1.2 million international students attending colleges and universities in the U.S. Due to information gap, many international students face considerable confusions with various issues critical to living in the U.S. For some of those issues, such as work authorization, necessary information is sometimes nonexistent. Even when the information is accessible, it is oftentimes difficult to comprehend. Information is the most important resource in this digital age, such insufficiency puts international students at a disadvantage, leading to the loss of opportunities when they are as qualified as their peers. Through this project, my team hopes to provide international students better access to comprehensible information needed to thrive in the U.S. and eventually help them fully realize their potentials.

Understanding Users

Contextual Interview

The goal for this project is to design a product to help international students overcome obstacles they encounter while studying and living in the States. It was crucial to do user research on international students to find out what their pain points and needs are. Our investigative process started with recruiting international students who were planning to work in the U.S. or were already actively seeking full time jobs in the U.S. My team prepared script for the interview protocol and the consent forms. We recruited 10 participants to conduct the interview.

Affinity diagram

After the interview study, we validated our assumption on that job search and work authorization is one of the most significant problems for many international students in the U.S., and this issue is greatly due to information gap. We also found that social life presented a problem space. The job search and work authorization issue is complex and significant enough, the social life issue may be too much of a complex issue to investigate within this project scope. Therefore, our team decided to focus our time and effort on exploring the problem space of job-related issues in the U.S.

We unpacked all 10 interview notes and collected 123 data points. We then categorized the data points and mapped them into an affinity diagram.

Key Findings

  • Participants had a basic understanding of how the work authorization process works in the U.S. and the corresponding requirements, but they were not fully aware of the details.
  • Some participants seemed frustrated about the technical terms (e.g. E-verify, OPT, visa sponsorship). The level of confidence about the technical terms was relatively low.
  • Some participants assumed that all big companies sponsor visas and are also E-verified, which is not true.
  • Most participants would obtain information about OPT and E-Verify from other people (e.g. friend, alumni, lawyer). They also found it was difficult to find understandable information on the details of work authorization in the U.S. (e.g. E-Verify).
  • Participants felt frustrated and insecure because there is no easy way to find out whether a company is E-Verified. Most of participants thought current job-searching platforms are lacking in “international-student-friendly” resources.

Requirements

We drafted some initial requirements for the system we were about to design after we grasped a better understanding of the problem context by unpacking the interview notes and creating the affinity diagram and flow model. With our system, the international students should be able to find:

  • Easily understandable information on work authorization (CTP, OPT, E-Verify, H-1B visa) in the U.S., and clear explanations of job-related requirements by reframing and visualizing the information.
  • An up-to-date and reliable list of U.S. employers who are willing to sponsor international students for work visa.
  • A list of jobs that are currently open for application with information on sponsorship highlighted.
  • Most current and reliable list of U.S. employers who participate in the E-Verify program.
  • A list of all events that are relevant to job-searching. The students shall be able to find details about all the career-related and networking events being organized on-campus.
  • Mentorship or peer-to-peer guidance.

Ideate

Brainstorming

The initial ideating phase involved brainstorming on different ideas to tackle the problem. Each member in the team brainstormed on 20 ideas, and the entire team generated about 100 ideas.

Sketches created during the brainstorming session.

Storyboards

After two rounds of ideations, we finalized five requirements for our system. The team made a consensus on which features to incorporate on the design:

  1. Crowdsourcing for E-verify
  2. Visualization of Work Authorization information
  3. Q & A for Peer-to-peer Mentorship
  4. Pinning and Checking
  5. Searchable and filterable job posting pages

We also drafted five user scenarios, and illustrated each scenario on storyboard.

Storyboards illustrate five user scenarios: 1. Company List and Page; 2. Work Authorization Education; 3. Question and Answer Forum; 4. Job-pinning Functionality; 5. Job List and Page.

Paper prototyping

Once we fleshed out some user scenarios, our team envisioned a website to assist international students with job searching process and clarify certain processes and terminologies about work authorization. This website is comprised of five parts, including job listing page and description page, company listing page and description page, work authorization training page, Q & A page, and the job-pinning functionality. We then built paper prototype incorporating all major functionalities to conduct usability testing.

Testing

We recruited three international students who were actively looking for jobs and asked the users to look at the animated movie of our paper prototype that illustrates the main functions step-by-step and to think out loud their confusions or comments on any of the features. Below are some key findings:

  • The knowledge of the job authorization requirements is critical to the understanding of our design. Therefore, we want to incorporate the educational information during the onboarding process.
  • The sponsorship and E-verify information caused a lot of confusions for our users. Consequently, we want to improve the way we display the E-verify and sponsorship information and also rethink the method of crowdsourcing that information.
  • Users showed little enthusiasm on the job posting page and company information page because of the similarity it possesses compared to existing solutions, but the pinning functionality was the highlight for all users. They appreciated the idea to be able to have a place to put all the job listings that they are following.

Since our system is specially designed with international students’ needs in mind, we wanted to find a way to strengthen the aspect of providing job authorization information to differentiate our design from other job searching platform. Because the pinning functionality was the most well-received feature among our users, we wanted to emphasis it. We made a pivot on the design to accommodate our new findings by highlighting the pinning functionality based on the feedbacks we received.

Iterating

The team iterated on the design focusing on the web extension and job pinning functionality. We then created low-fidelity Balsamiq prototype for another round of usability testing. The Balsamiq prototype is designed for the following primary tasks:

  • Get education on U.S. work authorization during onboarding.
  • Directly check the company’s E-Verify status in the browser extension.
  • Vote for company’s E-Verify status from the browser extension.
  • See sponsorship information and pin a job from other websites to the user’s personal portal and public board on our platform.
  • Organize pinned jobs collected from other websites (Handshake, Glassdoor, company website, etc.)

Evaluating

Heuristic evaluation

To self evaluate our design, the team conducted a heuristic evaluation based on Nielsen Norman's 10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design. We collected 57 entries of potential issues.

Images of heuristic evaluation problem report table

Design critique

During the design critique session, we received more positive feedback on the pinning functionality from our peers. This reassured our idea of changing the design to focus more on the web browser extension and job pinning functionality. We assumed that there would be few job pinnings at the beginning, but the number would gradually increase due to the crowdsourcing nature of the design. By having our users, who are mostly international students, generate all the postings on our public board, we hope to see a pool of jobs that better fits international students’ job-searching needs.

Usability testing

We conducted another rounds of usability testing to validate some assumptions about the new design approach. We wanted to check if the users can do the following:

  • Understand educational information about work authorization
  • Find reliable information on company's E-verify status and visa sponsorship status
  • Pin job posting to personal and public job boards
  • Organize job postings on personal board
  • Provide comments for a specific company

We drafted 4 tasks based on the functionalities we want our design to achieve, and then conducted the usability testing.

Findings

Here are some key findings we collected during the usability testing:

  • My Board: needs to better represent available actions for organizing job postings (now it's drag and drop which is not obvious)
  • Work Authorization Tutorial: educational information needs to be more concise and more cleanly presented
  • Extension: need confirmation after pinning
  • Extension: missing link to My Board
  • Extension: need better documentation/tutorial on the extension

Iterating

We went through another round of iteration and made improvements incorporating the findings from the usability testing.
